# Inspiration — References & Design Movements

## Design Movements to Study

### Swiss / International Typographic Style
Grids, sans-serifs, whitespace, objectivity. The origin of much of what bad design imitates without understanding.
- Study: Josef Müller-Brockmann's grid systems
- Typefaces born here: Helvetica, Univers, Akzidenz Grotesk

### Bauhaus (1919–1933)
Form follows function. Geometry. Primary colors used for reason, not decoration.
- Relevant now: the principle of honest materials applied to UI

### Emigre Magazine (1984–2005)
Raw, political, typographically experimental. Anti-slick.
- Typefaces: Matrix, Filosofia, Mrs Eaves — all have personality because they were made with a point of view

### Dutch Constructivism / De Stijl
Mondrian isn't decoration — it's a system. Understand why before stealing it.

### Brutalist Web Design (2010s revival)
Raw HTML aesthetics, visible structure, no ornament. Sites like Bloomberg Businessweek 2012–2016, Balenciaga.
- Key principle: the medium is visible, not hidden

### Editorial Print Design
Newspapers, magazines — masters of hierarchy, information density, and typographic rhythm under deadline pressure.
- Study: NYT Magazine, The Guardian, Le Monde, Süddeutsche Zeitung

## Typographers Worth Knowing

- **Jan Tschichold** — *The New Typography* (1928). Still required reading.
- **Erik Spiekermann** — Meta, FF Info. Type as communication system.
- **Herb Lubalin** — Avant Garde typeface, expressive display type.
- **Matthew Carter** — Georgia, Verdana, Tahoma. Digital type done right.
- **Kris Sowersby (Klim Type)** — Söhne, Canela, Tiempos. Contemporary rigor.
- **Paul Barnes / Christian Schwartz (Commercial Type)** — Guardian, Stag, Graphik.

## Digital Products That Avoided Slop

(For reference, not for copying)

- **Stripe (2012–2018)** — Before everyone copied it. Precise typography, real content, earned confidence.
- **Linear** — Keyboard-first, opinionated, fast. No dashboard bloat.
- **Notion (early)** — Block-based editing as the interface. Novel interaction as design.
- **Are.na** — Refuses to look like other platforms. Consistent aesthetic point of view.
- **Figma (canvas era)** — Infinite canvas as the metaphor for open-ended work.
- **iA Writer** — Typography IS the product. Nothing added that isn't typographic.

## Copy Writers Worth Studying

- **David Abbott** — Long-form print advertising. Read his Economist ads.
- **Luke Sullivan** — *Hey Whipple, Squeeze This*. Advertising honesty.
- **Paul Rand** — Logo and identity writing. Short, specific, confident.
- **The Economist style guide** — Clarity as discipline.
- **Ann Handley** — *Everybody Writes*. Applies to UX copy too.

## "Specific" as a Creative Constraint

The single most effective prompt for avoiding slop: **be specific**.

Instead of designing "a hero section", design:
> "A hero section for a solo bookkeeper who serves restaurants in one city, and whose biggest fear is looking unprofessional compared to big accounting firms."

The specificity forces every generic default out. You can't use the purple gradient when you have a real person in mind.

Apply this to:
- **Copy**: Write for one reader. Name them internally.
- **UI**: Design for one task, not for all possible tasks.
- **Color**: Reference a specific material, place, or time period — not "modern" or "clean."
- **Typography**: Ask what the typeface says before asking if it looks good.

## Anti-Patterns in the Wild (for training your eye)

Run through these sites and name every slop pattern you spot:
- Any AI startup landing page on Product Hunt (2024–2026)
- Any SaaS dashboard on Dribbble with 100k+ likes
- Any "UI kit" from Figma Community with "modern" in the title
- Any email from a newsletter that starts with "Hey [First Name],"

Then ask: what would I need to change to make this specific, intentional, and memorable?
